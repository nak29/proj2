Dot Com Bubble

In the 21st-century, people have come up with many ways of entertaining to fill up the fleeting free moments in our over-scheduled lives. There are many things that we love to do but above all, we love spending money. Some may say that our obsession with shopping reflects self-indulgence and luxury, really we are partaking in the evolution of capitalism. E-commerce is the new wave of capitalism, that is buying things online, transforming the technology of the World Wide Web into a Marketplace.

Jeff Bezos and Pierre Omidyar, the founders of Amazon and eBay, were some of the people that viewed the internet as a marketplace back in the 90s. Omidyar believed that he could use the power of the technology of the internet to bring people together into a marketplace using the market mechanism An opening that out to regular people, creating a level playing field where everyone could participate in the market. Omidyar believed that an auction-style site would be the fairest. Thanks to the explosiveness of the internet, eBay and Amazon were able to flourish.

This explosion fueled a mania amongst Americans and created a bubble. It was a modern-day Gold Rush. People poured money into the stock market and the stocks of these e-commerce companies. Any company with a dot com attached to its name, people wanted to invest in, after seeing the success of eBay and Amazon. In the same breath, there was a democratization of the stock market, removing the power from Wall Street brokers and giving access to the public.

However, every bubble must burst, and the first wave of e-commerce was a fragile one. Many e-commerce businesses that had been booming failed to draw in the necessary revenue during the holiday season. On April 14, 2000, the stock market crashed in the single greatest collapse in the history of the stock market.
